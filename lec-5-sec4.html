    Section 2.4 One-way ANOVA: Homogeneity of Variances

    For comparison of two population variances, recall that we have used the test statistic F such that
    \begin{equation*} F = \dfrac{s_1^2}{s_2^2}\sim F_{n_1-1,n_2-1} \end{equation*}
    under the null hypothesis \(H_0: \sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2\text{.}\) How do we generalize this to compare more than two variances?
    Consider the null
    \begin{equation*} H_0: \sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2=\cdots = \sigma_t^2 \end{equation*}
    versus the alternative \(H_a: \sigma_i^2\neq \sigma_j^2\) for some \(i,j\)
    One test is Hartley’s F-max test. It requires the balanced case, i.e. \(n_1 = \cdots = n_t = n\text{.}\) Let
    \begin{equation*} s_{max}^2 = \max_{1\leq i\leq t} s_i^2 \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} s_{min}^2 = \min_{1\leq i\leq t} s_i^2 \end{equation*}
    where \(s_i^2\) denotes the sample variance of the \(i-\)th treatment level.
    The test statistic can be given as
    \begin{equation*} F-max = \dfrac{s_{max}^2}{s_{min}^2}\sim F-max_{t,df} \text{ under }H_0 \end{equation*}
    where \(F-max_{t,df}\) is the F-max distribution (which is distinct from the usual F distribution) with \(t\) and \(df = n-1\text{.}\)
    We reject \(H_0: \sigma_1^2 = \cdots \sigma_t^2\) if \(F-max \geq F-max_{\alpha,t,df}\text{;}\) the critical value \(F-max_{\alpha,t,df}\) can be found in the F-max table.

    Example 2.4.1.

    An experiment to compare the yield of four varieties of rice was conducted. Each of 16 plots on a test farm where soil fertility was fairly homogeneous was treated alike relative to water and fertilizer. Four plots were randomly assigned each of the four varieties of rice.
    Variety Yields
    1 934 1041 1028 935
    2 880 963 924 946
    3 987 951 976 840
    4 992 1143 1140 1191
    Solution.
    Compute each variance:
    \begin{align*} s_1^2 &= 3361.67\\ s_2^2 &= 1289.58\\ s_3^2 &= 4539.00\\ s_4^2 &= 7435.00 \end{align*}
    and identify the values of \(s_{max}^2\) and \(s_{min}^2\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} s_{max}^2 & = 7435.00\\ s_{min}^2 & = 1289.58 \end{align*}
    Now, the \(F-max\) statistic is given by
    \begin{equation*} F-max = \dfrac{s_{max}^2}{s_{min}^2} = \dfrac{7435}{1289.58} = 5.77 \end{equation*}
    From the F-max table, we get
    \begin{equation*} F-max_{0.05,4,3} = 39.2 \end{equation*}
    Under
    \begin{equation*} H_0: \sigma_i^2 = \sigma_j^2 \text{ for all }i,j \end{equation*}
    we reject \(H_0\) if \(F-max \geq F-max_{0.05,4,3} \text{,}\) so we conclude there is insufficient evidence to reject the hypothesis of equal variances.
    Another test is Bartlett’s test. The test statistic is given by
    \begin{equation*} \chi^2 = \dfrac{1}{C}\left[ (n_\cdot - t)\log (MSE) - \sum_{i=1}^t (n_i-1)s_i^2\right] \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{equation*} C = 1 + \dfrac{1}{3(t-1)} \lrpar{\sum_{i=1}^t \dfrac{1}{n_i-1} - \dfrac{1}{n_\cdot - t}} \end{equation*}
    For this test, we don’t require the balanced case. Additionally, the test statistic approximately follows \(\chi^2_{t-1}\) under the null hypothesis. We set
    \begin{equation*} H_0: \sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2=\cdots = \sigma_t^2 \end{equation*}
    and reject if \(\chi^2 \geq \chi^2_{\alpha,t-1}\text{.}\)
    Note: Bartlett’s test is sensitive to departures from the normality assumption.
    A third test is Levene’s test: let
    \begin{equation*} r_{ij} = |\hat{\varepsilon_{ij}}| = |Y_{ij} - \overline{Y}_{i\cdot}| \end{equation*}
    denote the absolute value of the residual \(\hat{\varepsilon_{ij}}\text{.}\) We perform one-way ANOVA by treating the \(r_{ij}\) as our observations.
    Levene’s test is based on the F test statistic:
    \begin{equation*} F = \dfrac{\sum_{i=1}^t n_i(\overline{r}_{i\cdot} - \overline{r}_{\cdot\cdot})^2/(t-1)}{\sum_{i=1}^t\sum_{j=1}^{n_i} (r_{ij}-\overline{r}_{i\cdot})^2/(n_\cdot - t)}\sim F_{t-1,n_\cdot-t} \end{equation*}
    under \(H_0\text{,}\) where
    \begin{equation*} \overline{r}_{i\cdot} = \dfrac{1}{n_i}\sum_{j=1}^{n_i} r_{ij} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \overline{r}_{\cdot\cdot} = \dfrac{1}{n_\cdot} \sum_{i=1}^t\sum_{j=1}^{n_i}r_{ij} \end{equation*}
    We reject the null hypothesis of equal variance if \(F\geq F_{\alpha,t-1,n_\cdot -t}\)
    Once we conduct the hypothesis testing for homogeneity of variances, we consider the following:
    • If we fail to reject the null, we go with one-way ANOVA
    • If we reject the null, we have two choices:
      • Apply Welch’s ANOVA with each \(s_i^2\) instead of the pooled variance. Calculate the degrees of freedom for error just like what we did in the two-sample t-test for unequal variances
      • Perform data transformation to stabilize the variance
