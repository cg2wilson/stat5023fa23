    Section 12.1 Latin Square Design

    Subsection 12.1.1 Latin Square Design

    Recall that in RCBD, we had one factor chosen to be blocked in order to control outside variability. A Latin Square design chooses to block two factors. The two sources of variability are represented as rows and columns; each treatment level occurs once and only once in each row and column. This procedure requires that there be exactly \(t\) rows and columns, for the \(t\) treatments.
    As a quick example, consider this situation: A consumer organization wants to evaluate three major brands, \(B_1,B_2,B_3\text{,}\) of room air cleaners. In order to make the ratings as realistic as possible, they choose to evaluate the air cleaners outside the testing laboratory in residential homes. To control for variations due to the differening air qualities in the homes and due to the time-of-year characteristics of external air pollution, they use a cleaner of each brand in each of three homes and to run the tests at three different months. This would result in the following square:
    Home 1 Home 2 Home 3
    Month 1 \(B_1\) \(B_2\) \(B_3\)
    Month 2 \(B_2\) \(B_3\) \(B_1\)
    Month 3 \(B_3\) \(B_1\) \(B_2\)
    For Latin Squares, proper random assignment of treatment matters. We may randomly assign some labels to columns in Row 1, then assign them to remaining rows so that each label appears only once in each row and column. Then, we randonmly assign the treatment levels to each label.
    The statistical effects model is given by
    \begin{equation*} Y_{ij(k)} = \mu + \rho_i + \gamm_j + \tau_k + \varepsilon_{ijk} \end{equation*}
    for \(i,j,k=1,...,t\) with
    • \(Y_{ij(k)}\) being the observation at row \(i\) and column \(j\) with treatment level \(k\)
    • \(\mu\) the overall mean
    • \(\rho_i\) the effect of row \(i\) such that \(\rho_i\sim^{iid} N(0,\sigma_{row}^2)\)
    • \(\gamma_j\) the effect of row \(j\) such that \(\gamma_j\sim^{iid} N(0,\sigma_{column}^2)\)
    • \(\tau_k\) is a fixed (non-random) effect of treatment level \(k\)
    • \(\varepsilon_{ijk}\sim^{iid} N(0,\sigma_\varepsilon^2)\) is a random error.
    We have the following source of variation in a Latin Square Design:
    Source df EMS \(H_0\) \(F\)
    Row \(t-1\) \(\sigma_\varepsilon^2 + t\sigma^2_{row}\)
    Column \(t-1\) \(\sigma_\varepsilon^2 + t\sigma^2_{column}\)
    Treatment \(t-1\) \(\sigma_\varepsilon^2 + \dfrac{t}{t-1}\sum_{k=1}^t \tau_k^2\) \(H_0: \tau_1 = \cdots = \tau_t = 0\) \(\dfrac{MSTrt}{MSE}\)
    Error \((t-1)(t-2)\) \(\sigma_\varepsilon^2\)
    Total \(t^2-1\)
    Note the following:
    • The treatment mean is given by
      \begin{equation*} \overline{Y}_k = \dfrac{1}{t} \sum_{i,j} Y_{ij(k)} \end{equation*}
    • \(\displaystyle Var(\overline{Y}_k) = \dfrac{1}{t}\lrpar{\sigma_{row}^2 + \sigma^2_{column} + \sigma^2_\varepsilon}\)
    • The standard error estimate of \(\overline{Y}_k\) is given by
      \begin{equation*} s_{\overline{Y}_k} = \sqrt{Var(\overline{Y}_k)} \end{equation*}
    Also, \(\tau_k - \tau_{k'}\) can be estimated by \(\overline{Y}_k - \overline{Y}_{k'}\text{.}\) This means that
    • \(\displaystyle Var(\overline{Y}_k - \overline{Y}_{k'}) = \dfrac{2}{t}\sigma_\varepsilon^2\)
    • \(\displaystyle s_{\overline{Y}_k - \overline{Y}_{k'}} = \sqrt{Var(\overline{Y}_k - \overline{Y}_{k'})}\)
    • A \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) CI for \(\tau_k-\tau_{k'}\) is given by
      \begin{equation*} (Var(\overline{Y}_k - \overline{Y}_{k'})) \pm t_{\alpha/2,(t-1)(t-2)}\cdot s_{Var(\overline{Y}_k - \overline{Y}_{k'})} \end{equation*}
    GIven the above estimated standard errors, we can make inferences using contrasts and multiple comparisons. Let \(L = \ds \sum_{k=1}^t a_k\tau_k\) be a contrast of interest. Then,
    • A \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) CI for \(L\) is given by
      \begin{equation*} \ds \sum_{k=1}^t a_k\overline{Y}_k\pm t_{\alpha/2,(t-1)(t-2)}\cdot \sqrt{\hat{\sigma}^2_\varepsilon \sum_{k=1}^t \dfrac{1}{t}a_k^2} \end{equation*}
      Note that \(\ds \sum_{k=1}^t a_k\overline{Y}_k\) is \(\hat{L}\) and \(s_{\hat{L}}\) is given by \(\sqrt{\hat{\sigma}^2_\varepsilon \sum_{k=1}^t \dfrac{1}{t}a_k^2}\)
    • We may hypothesis test \(H_0: L = 0\) vs. \(H_a: L\neq 0\) using a \(t-\)test or \(F-\)test as before:
      \begin{equation*} t = \dfrac{\sum a_k \overline{Y}_k}{\sqrt{\hat{\sigma}^2_\varepsilon \sum \dfrac{1}{t}a_k^2}}\sim t_{(t-1)(t-2)} \end{equation*}
      or
      \begin{equation*} F = \dfrac{SSC_L/1}{MSE}\sim F_{\alpha,1,(t-1)(t-2)} \end{equation*}
      where \(SSC_L = \dfrac{\hat{L}^2}{\sum \dfrac{1}{t} a_k^2} = \dfrac{t\lrpar{\sum a_k\overline{Y}_k}^2}{\sum a_k^2}\text{.}\) Note that we reject \(H_0\) if
      \begin{equation*} |t| \geq t_{\alpha/2,(t-1)(t-2)} \end{equation*}
      or
      \begin{equation*} F\geq F_{\alpha,1,(t-1)(t-2)} \end{equation*}
    When two sources of outside variability can be well-identified, the Latin square is an effective design. We only restrict that the number of treatments be equal to the number of rows and columns. Small designs generally have too few replications for a desirable level of precision, however.

    Subsection 12.1.2 Latin Square Design with Multiple Squares

    Consider a \(2\times 2\) Latin square:
    Source df
    Row 1
    Column 1
    Treatment 1
    Error 0
    Total 3
    Here, there are no degrees of freedom for error; this means that no test of significance can be done. For this reason, more than one Latin square can be used in an experiment. The larger df means that the have a better estimate of the error variance.
    For \(s\) many \(t\times t\) Latin squares, the sources of variation can generally be partitioned as follows:
    Source df
    Square \(s\)
    Row within Square \(s(t-1)\)
    Column within Square \(s(t-1)\)
    Treatment \(t-1\)
    Error \(s(t-1)(t-2)+(s-1)(t-1)\)
    Total \(t^2s-1\)
    As an example, assume that there are two lab technicians to carry out an experiment. Each of the technicians has different "luck" in carrying out the experiment.
    Technician 1
    Oven 1 Oven 2 Oven 3 Oven 4
    Day 1 A B D C
    Day 2 D C A B
    Day 3 C A B D
    Day 4 B D C A
    Technician 2
    Oven 5 Oven 6 Oven 7 Oven 8
    Day 5 B D A C
    Day 6 A C B D
    Day 7 D A C B
    Day 8 C B D A
