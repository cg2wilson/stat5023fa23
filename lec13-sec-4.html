    Section 6.4 Models for Factorial Experiments and Connections to ANOVA

    A completely randomized design with \(a\times b\) factorial treatment structure can be described in terms of a statistical model as follows:
    \begin{align*} \text{Statistical Means Model: } \amp Y_{ijk} = \mu_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ijk} \\ \text{Statistical Effects Model: } \amp Y_{ijk} = \mu + \alpha_i + \beta_j + (\alpha\beta)_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ijk} \end{align*}
    where
    • \(Y_{ijk}\) is the k-th observation at \(A=i,B=j\)
    • \(\mu_{ij}\) is the average of the population with \(A=i,B=j\)
    • \(\mu\) is the overall population mean
    • \(\alpha_i\) is the average of the population with \(A = i\)
    • \(\beta_j\) is the average of the population with \(B= j\)
    • \((\alpha\beta)_{ij}\) is the interaction between two populations with \(A=i,B=j\)
    • \(\varepsilon_{ijk}\sim^{iid} N(0,\sigma^2)\) is a random error
    Reconsider the car/gasoline example as a one-way treatment structure with six levels (three car models and two additives); the data is reproduced below:
    \(A = 1\) \(A =2 \) \(A = 3\)
    \(B = 1\) \(23, 24\) \(28, 29\) \(27, 24\)
    \(B =2 \) \(24, 21\) \(31, 31\) \(23, 24\)
    We have the one-way ANOVA table:
    Source df SS MS F \(Pr \gt F\)
    Trt 5 107.6667 21.5333 10.7 0.0059
    Error 6 12.0000 2.0
    Total 11 119.6667
    From this, we can test
    \begin{equation*} H_0: \mu_{11} = \mu_{12} = \mu_{21} = \mu_{22} = \mu_{31} = \mu_{32} \end{equation*}
    against \(H_a: \mu_{ij}\neq \mu_{(ij)'}\) for at least one pair \(i,j\text{.}\) Under \(\alpha = 0.05\text{,}\) we conclude that there is a significant difference in average of mpg among the six treatment combinations.
    So, we construct five meaningful, mutually orthogonal contrasts as follows:
    \(\mu_{11}\) \(\mu_{12}\) \(\mu_{21}\) \(\mu_{22}\) \(\mu_{31}\) \(\mu_{32}\) \(L\)
    1 1 1 0 0 \(-1\) \(-1\) \(\mu_{11} + \mu_{12} - (\mu_{31} + \mu_{32})\)
    2 1 1 \(-2\) \(-2\) \(1\) \(1\) \(\mu_{11} + \mu_{12} - 2(\mu_{21} + \mu_{22}) + \mu_{31} + \mu_{32}\)
    3 1 \(-1\) 1 \(-1\) \(1\) \(-1\) \(\mu_{11} - \mu_{12} + \mu_{21} - \mu_{22}+ \mu_{31} - \mu_{32}\)
    4 1 \(-1\) 0 0 \(-1\) \(1\) \(\mu_{11} - \mu_{12} - \mu_{31} + \mu_{32}\)
    5 1 \(-1\) \(-2\) \(2\) \(1\) \(-1\) \(\mu_{11} - \mu_{12} - 2\mu_{21} + 2\mu_{22} + \mu_{31} - \mu_{32}\)
    The description of the contrasts are given:
    • Contrast 1: Difference in average mpg between Car 1 and Car 3
    • Contrast 2: Difference between average mpg of Cars 1 & 3 and that of Car 2
    • Contrast 3: Difference in average mpg between Additive 1 and Additive 2
    • Contrast 4: Difference in average mpg between Car 1 and Car 3 for each additive
    • Contrast 5: Difference between average mpg of Cars 1 & 3 and that of Car 2 for each Additive.
    Contrasts 1 and 2 give a model of car effect; contrast 3 gives an effect of additive; contrasts 3 and 4 give interaction between car and additive.
    The following table shows the corresponding \(\hat{L}\text{,}\) \(SSC_{L}\) and \(F\)-statistic values:
    Contrast \(\hat{L}\) \(SSC_L\)
    \(1\) \(-2.5\) \(3.125\)
    \(2\) \(23.5\) \(92.04167\)
    \(3\) \(1.0\) \(0.3333\)
    \(4\) \(-0.5\) \(0.125\)
    \(5\) \(8.5\) \(12.0417\)
    Note that \(SSTrt = 107.6667\text{.}\) The SS due to the model of car (\(SSA\))is 95.16667 with \(df = 2\text{,}\) the SS due to gasoline additive (\(SSB\)) is 0.333 with \(df=1\text{,}\) and the SS due to interaction (\(SS(AB)\)) is 12.16667 with \(df = 2\)
    The decomposition of SSTrt via each contrast gives the following table:
    Source df SS MS F \(Pr \gt F\)
    Car 2 95.1667 47.83335 23.7917 0.0014
    Additive 1 0.3333 0.3333 0.1665 0.6973
    Interaction 2 12.1667 6.08335 3.0417 0.1224
    Error 6 12.0000 2.0
    Total 11 1119.6667
    We conclude that there is no significant interaction between model of car and gasoline additive under \(\alpha = 0.05\text{,}\) and the effect of gasoline additive is not significant under the same significance. However, we do conclude that the effect of the car model is significant.
    For a CRD of \(a\times b\) factorial treatment structure with the same number of replicates \(n\text{,}\) the source of variation can be decomposed as follows:
    Source df SS MS F
    A a-1 SSA MSA = SSA/(a-1) MSA/MSE
    B b-1 SSB MSB = SSB/(b-1) MSB/MSE
    AB (a-1)(b-1) SS(AB) MSA = SS(AB)/((a-1)(b-1)) MS(AB)/MSE
    Error ab(n-1) SSE MSE = SSE/(ab(n-1))
    Total abn-1 SSTotal
    When conducting experiments using ANOVA for factorial experiments, we have the following options:
    Means Model Effects Model Test Statistic
    \(H_0:\overline{\mu_{1\cdot}} = \cdots \overline{\mu_{a\cdot}}\) \(H_0: \alpha_1 = \cdots = \alpha_a = 0\) \(F = MSA/MSE\)
    \(H_a:\) at least one \(\overline{\mu_{i\cdot}}\) is distinct \(H_a:\) at least one \(\alpha_{i}\) is distinct Reject \(H_0\) if \(F\geq F_{\alpha,a-1,ab(n-1)}\)
    \(H_0: \overline{\mu_{\cdot 1}} = \cdots \overline{\mu_{\cdot b}}\) \(H_0: \beta_1 = \cdots = \beta_b = 0\) \(F = MSB/MSE\)
    \(H_a:\) at least one \(\overline{\mu_{\cdot j}}\) is distinct \(H_a:\) at least one \(\beta_j\) is distinct Reject \(H_0\) if \(F\geq F_{\alpha,b-1,ab(n-1)}\)
    \(H_0: \mu_{ij} - \mu_{ij'} = \mu_{i'j}-\mu_{i'j'} \forall i\neq i', j\neq j'\) \(H_0: (\alpha\beta)_{ij} = 0 \forall i,j\) \(F = MS(AB)/MSE\)
    \(H_a: \mu_{ij} - \mu_{ij'} \neq \mu_{i'j}-\mu_{i'j'}\) for at least one \(i\neq i',j\neq j'\) \(H_a:\) at least one \((\alpha\beta)_{ij}\) is nonzero Reject \(H_0\) if \(F\geq F_{\alpha,(a-1)(b-1),ab(n-1)}\)
    Here, \(\ds\overline{\mu_{i\cdot}} = \dfrac{1}{b} \sum_{j=1}^b \mu_{ij}\) and \(\ds \overline{\mu_{\cdot j}}\dfrac{1}{a}\sum_{i=1}^a \mu_{ij}\)
    When conducting these tests, we should first conduct a test for interaction between two factors. If significant interaction exists, the tests for main effects must be interpreted carefully. This is because the main effects of some treatments are no longer constant for the levels of another treatment under significant interaction between A and B. Thus, under significant interaction, the means of treatment A need to be compared for each level of treatment B.
    In the absence of significant interaction, the test for main effects can be interpreted separately.
    Ultimately, this gives some steps, given a 2-way ANOVA table:
    1. Check the F-statistic for interaction
    2. If the resulting interaction is significant, then compare the average case-by-case; make a comparison for each group. For example, in the car-gas additive example, if there is significant interaction, we will need to compare the means:
      \begin{align*} H_{0}: \amp \mu_{11} - \mu{21} = 0 \\ H_{0}: \amp \mu_{11} - \mu{32} = 0 \\ H_{0}: \amp \mu_{21} - \mu{31} = 0 \\ H_{0}: \amp \mu_{12} - \mu{22} = 0 \\ H_{0}: \amp \mu_{12} - \mu{32} = 0 \\ H_{0}: \amp \mu_{22} - \mu{13} = 0 \end{align*}
    3. If there is not significant interaction, we can make an interpretation from the first two rows of the table (the treatment rows) for main effect.
