    Section 16.1 Analysis of Covariance

    Subsection 16.1.1 Motivation

    With one-way ANOVA in a CRD, we look at a comparison of a single treatment with \(t\) levels; we implicitly assume that we have homogeneous experimental units. However, often there are additional sources of variability which impact the response. In RCBD, we require a correct identification of blocking factor; what if we have a continuous blocking factor instead? RCBD requires a discrete factor, so you would have to introduce a level of subjectivity.
    Suppose that in addition to a response \(Y\text{,}\) we observe some auxiliary variable \(X\text{.}\) We call \(X\) covariate and consider it to be lienarly related to \(Y\text{;}\) covariation is difficult to control in experimentation.
    ANCOVA makes an adjustment of \(X\) to remove the additional source of variability by \(X\) that would be associated with \(Y\text{.}\) It involves fitting several linear lines to observations at each treatment level to account for the effect of the covariate \(X\) and often leads to more precise statistical inference of treatment effects.

    Example 16.1.1.

    The effects of a slow-release fertilizer (S) and a fast-release fertilizer (F) on seed yield of peanut plants were compared to a control (C), which is a standard fertilizer, with 10 replications of each treatment. The researcher recognized that the 30 plants were not at the same level of development thus their heights were recorded at the start of the experiment.

    Subsection 16.1.2 Using ANCOVA

    We can consider the following two models with a covariate in addition to the ANOVA model for a one-way treatment structure in a CRD:
    Parallel Lines Model:
    \begin{equation*} Y_{ij} = \alpha_i + \beta X_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ij} \end{equation*}
    for \(i=1,...,t\text{,}\) \(j=1,...,n\text{,}\) where
    • \(Y_{ij}\) denotes response for the \(j-\)th replicate of treatment level \(i\)
    • \(\alpha_i\text{:}\) the \(y-\)intercept for treatment level \(i\)
    • \(\beta\text{:}\) the slope (same for all treatment levels)
    • \(X_{ij}\text{:}\) covariate for \(j-\)th replicate of treatment level \(i\)
    • \(\varepsilon_{ij}\text{:}\) random error associated with \(Y_{ij}\) such that \(\varepsilon\overline{\sim}{iid}N(0,\sigma^2_\varepsilon)\)
    Non-Parallel Lines Model:
    \begin{equation*} Y_{ij} = \alpha_i + \beta X_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ij} \end{equation*}
    for \(i=1,...,t\text{,}\) \(j=1,...,n\text{,}\) where
    • \(Y_{ij}\) denotes response for the \(j-\)th replicate of treatment level \(i\)
    • \(\alpha_i\text{:}\) the \(y-\)intercept for treatment level \(i\)
    • \(\beta_i\text{:}\) the slope for treatment level \(i\)
    • \(X_{ij}\text{:}\) covariate for \(j-\)th replicate of treatment level \(i\)
    • \(\varepsilon_{ij}\text{:}\) random error associated with \(Y_{ij}\) such that \(\varepsilon\stackrel{iid}{\sim}N(0,\sigma^2_\varepsilon)\)
    Step-by-step analysis goes as follows:
    1. Is the covariate necessary? Test
      \begin{equation*} H_0: \beta_1 = \cdots = \beta_t = 0 \end{equation*}
      vs.
      \begin{equation*} H_a: \beta_j \neq 0 \text{ for some } j \end{equation*}
      If we fail to reject \(H_0\text{,}\) we proceed with one-way ANOVA. If we reject \(H_0\text{,}\) proceed to step 2.
    2. Are the lines parallel? Test
      \begin{equation*} H_0: \beta_1 = \cdots = \beta_t \end{equation*}
      vs.
      \begin{equation*} H_a: \text{ at least one }\beta_i\text{ is distinct} \end{equation*}
      If we fail to reject \(H_0\text{,}\) we adopt the parallel lines model. Then, each treatment effect can be compared at any value of the covariate. This is equivalent to testing
      \begin{equation*} H_0: \alpha_1 = \cdots = \alpha_t \end{equation*}
      vs.
      \begin{equation*} H_a: \text{ at least one }\alpha_i \text{ is distinct} \end{equation*}
      If we reject \(H_0\text{,}\) we adopt the non-parallel lines model. Then, each treatment effect needs to be compared at different values of the covariates, which is equivalent to testing
      \begin{equation*} H_0: \alpha_1 + \beta_1x_0 = \cdots = \alpha_t + \beta_t x_0 \end{equation*}
      vs.
      \begin{equation*} H_a:\text{ at least one }\alpha_i + \beta_i x_0\text{ is distinct} \end{equation*}
      where \(x_0\) is some specific value of \(X\text{.}\)
