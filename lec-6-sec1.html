    Section 3.1 Linear Contrasts

    Suppose that we reject the null hypothesis of equal variances under ANOVA. This means that we believe the alternative hypothesis that at least one \(\mu_i\) is different- which group means are different?

    Definition 3.1.1. Linear Contrast.

    A linear combination of the population group means, that is,
    \begin{equation*} L = \sum_{i=1}^t a_i\mu_i \end{equation*}
    is called a linear contrast of \(\mu_1,\mu_2,...,\mu_t\) if
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^t a_i = 0 \end{equation*}

    Example 3.1.2.

    An experiment to compare the yield of four varieties of rice was conducted.
    Case 1: Assume we are interested in any difference between the means of the first and fourth varieties.
    Case 2: Suppose that the fourth variety is a new type of rice and we want to know whether its mean yield is different from the average of the means for the other three varieties.
    Our hypotheses in either case are
    \begin{equation*} H_0: L = \sum_{i=1}^t a_i\mu_i = 0 \end{equation*}
    versus
    \begin{equation*} H_a: L = \sum_{i=1}^t a_i\mu_i \neq 0 \end{equation*}
    Solution.
    Case 1: With \(a_1 = 1\text{,}\) \(a_2=a_3 = 0\) and \(a_4=-1\text{,}\) the contrast
    Case 2: \(a_1 = a_2=a_3 = \dfrac{1}{3}\) and \(a_4 = -1\text{,}\) so the contrast
    \begin{equation*} L = \dfrac{1}{3}(\mu_1 + \mu_2 + \mu_3) - \mu_4 \end{equation*}
    can be considered
    Here are some useful formulas to construct the test statistic. When \(Y_1,Y_2,...,Y_n\) is a random sample from a population with variance \(\sigma^2\text{,}\) we have
    • \(\displaystyle \text{Var}\lrpar{\sum_{i=1}^n Y_i} = \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(Y_i) = \sum_{i=1}^n \sigma^2 = n\sigma^2\)
    • \(\displaystyle \text{Var}\lrpar{\sum_{i=1}^n a_iY_i} = \sum_{i=1}^na_i^2\text{Var}(Y_i) = \sigma^2\sum_{i=1}^n a_i^2\)
    • \(\displaystyle \text{Var}(\overline{Y}) = \text{Var}\lrpar{\dfrac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n Y_i} = \sum_{i=1}^n \dfrac{1}{n^2}\text{Var}(Y_i) = \dfrac{1}{n^2}\cdot n\sigma^2 = \dfrac{\sigma^2}{n}\)
    These formulas hold without normality as well.
    Set
    \begin{equation*} \hat{L} = \sum_{i=1}^t a_i\overline{Y}_{i\cdot} \end{equation*}
    to be the estimate of \(L\text{.}\) Under the assumptions of one-way ANOVA,
    \begin{equation*} \hat{L} \sim N\lrpar{\sum_{i=1}^t a_i\mu_i,\text{Var}(\hat{L})} \end{equation*}
    where
    \begin{equation*} \text{Var}(\hat{L}) = \sigma^2\sum_{i=1}^t \dfrac{a_i^2}{n_i} \end{equation*}
    (using the above formulas to simplify along the way). Without the knowledge of \(\sigma^2\text{,}\) \(\text{Var}(\hat{L})\) can be estimated by \(s_{\hat{L}}^2\) such that
    \begin{equation*} s_{\hat{L}} = \sqrt{MSE \sum_{i=1}^t \dfrac{a_i^2}{n_i}} \end{equation*}
    The test statistic is given by
    \begin{equation*} t = \dfrac{\hat{L}}{s_{\hat{L}}} = \dfrac{\sum_{i=1}^t a_i\overline{Y}_{i\cdot}}{\sqrt{MSE \sum_{i=1}^t \dfrac{a_i^2}{n_i}}} \sim t_{n_\cdot -t} \end{equation*}
    under \(H_0\text{.}\) We reject the null \(H_0: L = 0\) if \(|t| \geq t_{\alpha/2,n_\cdot-t}\text{.}\)
    A \(100(1-\alpha)\%\) confidence interval for \(L\) is given by
    \begin{equation*} \sum_{i=1}^t a_i\overline{Y}_{i\cdot} \pm t_{\alpha/2,n_\cdot -t} \sqrt{MSE \sum_{i=1}^t \dfrac{a_i^2}{n_i}} \end{equation*}
    It can be shown that \(t_{n_\cdot-t}^2 = F_{1,n_\cdot-t}\text{;}\) so, if we have a statistic from a t-distribution with \(\nu\) degrees of freeom, its square is from the F-distribution with 1 and \(\nu\) degrees of freedom. THis gives an alternative F-test statistic:
    \begin{equation*} F = t^2 = \dfrac{SSC_L/1}{MSE}\sim F_{1,n_\cdot-t} \end{equation*}
    under \(H_0\text{,}\) where \(t = \hat{L}/s_{\hat{L}}\) and \(SSC_L\) is referred to as the sum of squares for the contrast \(L\) such that
    \begin{equation*} SSC_L = \dfrac{\lrpar{\sum_{i=1}^t a_i\overline{Y}_{i \cdot}}^2}{\sum_{i=1}^t \dfrac{a_i^2}{n_i}} \end{equation*}
    \(SSC_L\) has one degree of freedom (this is the reason for the division by 1 above); we reject the null \(H_0: L = 0\) if \(F\geq F_{\alpha,1,n_\cdot -t}\)
    We may ask: why is the rejection region of the F-statistic only one-sided, even though the t-statistic is two-sided?
    • Recall that we test \(H_0: L = 0\) based on the t-statistic following the t-distribution, which is defined on \((-\infty,\infty)\)
    • We reject \(H_0\) if the t-statistic is too large in magnitude.
    • If we square a significant t-statistic, we get two situations: (1), the resulting F-statistic would be large with positive sign; (2), the F-distribution then becomes defined on \((0,\infty)\)
    • As a result, we only need to consider the right-hand region on the F distribution.
    • A small F-statistic would correspond to a t-statistic with small absolute value \(|t|\text{,}\) which should not lead us to reject the null. Thus, there is no need to consider the left part of the F distribution.

    Example 3.1.3.

    Researchers conducted a study of biological agents to assess their effectiveness in comparison to the chemical weed agents. The study consisted of a control (no agent), two biological agents (Bio1 and Bio2) and two chemical agents (Chm1 and Chm2). Thirty 1-acre plots of land were planted with hay. Six plots were randomly assigned to receive one of the five treatments. The hay was harvested, and the total yield in tons per acre was recorded for each plot.
    None (Control) Bio1 Bio2 Chm1 Chm2
